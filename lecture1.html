Lecture 1: Microcontrollers, Sensors, and Actuators

Note

This is a pre-course lecture. Watch the recorded video, then complete the Lecture 1 quiz on Canvas before the course starts.

Learning objectives

By the end of this lecture you should be able to:

  • Explain what a microcontroller is and what it contains
  • Describe what the BBC micro:bit is and identify its main components
  • Distinguish between a sensor and an actuator
  • Distinguish between a digital and an analog signal
  • Give examples of sensors and actuators relevant to medicine and agriculture

1. What is a microcontroller?

Think about what a doctor does. They receive information — a patient’s temperature, pulse, breathing rate — they process it, make a decision, and then act: prescribing a drug, adjusting a drip, raising an alarm.

A microcontroller does exactly this, but with electricity instead of clinical judgment.

A microcontroller is a small, self-contained computer on a single chip. It has three essential parts:

  • A processor — the part that runs instructions, one step at a time
  • Memory — where the program and temporary data are stored
  • Input/output pins — physical connections to the outside world, where signals come in from sensors and go out to actuators

Microcontrollers are everywhere: in insulin pumps, pulse oximeters, irrigation timers, incubators, and refrigerators that keep vaccines cold. They are cheap, low-power, and reliable — which makes them ideal for low-resource settings.

2. The BBC micro:bit

The micro:bit is a small microcontroller board designed for learning. It is about the size of a credit card. We will use it throughout this course.

It contains:

  • A processor and memory — the micro:bit V2 has a 64 MHz ARM processor and 512 KB of flash memory
  • A 5×5 LED matrix — 25 small lights that can display text, numbers, and simple images
  • Two buttons (A and B) — physical inputs you can press
  • An accelerometer — measures movement and tilt
  • A compass / magnetometer — detects magnetic fields and orientation
  • A temperature sensor — built into the processor chip
  • A microphone and speaker (V2 only)
  • Bluetooth and radio — for wireless communication between micro:bits or with a phone
  • Edge connector pins — 25 pins along the bottom edge for connecting external sensors and actuators

The edge connector is what makes the micro:bit extensible. By plugging in a breakout board — like the Elecfreaks Sensorbit we use in this course — you can connect many different sensors and actuators without soldering.

3. Signals: digital and analog

Before we talk about sensors and actuators, we need to understand two kinds of signals.

Digital signals have only two states: on or off, 1 or 0, high or low. A light switch is digital — it is either up or down, nothing in between. A button press is digital. A motion detector that says “motion detected / no motion” is digital.

Analog signals exist on a continuous scale. A dimmer switch is analog — you can set the light to any brightness between fully off and fully on. Temperature is analog — it can be 21.3°C, 21.35°C, or 37.2°C. Analog signals are converted to numbers by an analog-to-digital converter (ADC), which samples the signal and turns it into a value the processor can work with.

Why does this matter? Because sensors produce different kinds of signals, and you need to read them correctly in your program.

4. Sensors

A sensor is a device that measures something in the physical world and converts it into an electrical signal.

Sensor What it measures Signal type Example use
DHT11 Temperature and humidity Digital (serial) Monitor storage conditions for vaccines or grain
Soil moisture Water content in soil Analog Trigger irrigation when soil is too dry
PIR Movement of warm bodies Digital Detect a patient entering a room; scare animals from crops
MQ3 alcohol Alcohol vapor in air Analog Screen for alcohol in breath; monitor fermentation
UV Ultraviolet light intensity Analog Measure sun exposure; optimise plant growth
Crash sensor Physical contact / pressure Digital Detect if a door or container has been opened

Some important things to understand about sensors:

  • Every sensor has a range. The DHT11 measures temperature between 0°C and 50°C. Outside that range, the reading is wrong or absent. Choosing the right sensor for your use case means checking the range.
  • Every sensor has accuracy limits. The DHT11 is accurate to ±2°C. For monitoring a patient’s fever that may be acceptable; for calibrating a laboratory instrument it is not.
  • Sensors need time. The MQ3 alcohol sensor requires a warm-up period before its readings are reliable. The DHT11 can only be read once every two seconds.
  • Sensors can fail or drift. A soil moisture sensor left in the ground for months may corrode and give false readings.

Evaluating a sensor means asking: does it measure the right thing, at the right range, with enough accuracy, reliably enough, for my use case?

5. Actuators

An actuator is a device that converts an electrical signal into a physical action.

Actuator What it does Example use
LED / Rainbow LED Emits light in different colours Status indicator; phototherapy prototype
OLED screen Displays text and graphics Show readings to a user
Servo motor (180°) Rotates to a specific angle Open a valve; move a flap
DC motor with fan Spins continuously Ventilation; cooling

Actuators are the outputs of your system. A well-designed device combines sensors with actuators: a temperature sensor detects that a storage room is too hot, and a fan actuator turns on to cool it down.

6. Putting it together: sense → decide → act

Every microcontroller-based system follows the same basic pattern:

  1. A sensor measures something and sends a signal to the microcontroller
  2. The processor runs your program, which reads the signal and decides what to do
  3. The program sends a signal to an actuator, which does something in the world

Example: A simple hand sanitiser dispenser for a clinic entrance.

  • A PIR sensor detects that someone has approached
  • The micro:bit detects the signal and checks: “is there motion?”
  • If yes, it sends a signal to a servo motor that presses the sanitiser pump

This is not a complicated program. It is a real product. This is what we will learn to build.

7. Why this matters in medicine and agriculture

Microcontrollers are particularly valuable in settings where:

  • Continuous monitoring is needed but trained staff are limited
  • Automation can reduce human error
  • Cost is a constraint
  • Power supply may be intermittent

In Uganda and across sub-Saharan Africa, there are real problems these tools can address:

  • Cold chain monitoring for vaccines and blood products
  • Automated irrigation for small-scale farms
  • Environmental monitoring in patient wards
  • Early warning systems for crop disease or pest activity
  • Point-of-care diagnostic aids

This course will not solve all of these problems. But by the end of it, you will have the practical knowledge to begin.

Summary

  • A microcontroller has a processor, memory, and input/output pins
  • The BBC micro:bit is a microcontroller with many built-in sensors and a connector for external ones
  • Digital signals have two states; analog signals are continuous
  • Sensors convert physical phenomena into electrical signals
  • Actuators convert electrical signals into physical action
  • Every system follows the pattern: sense → decide → act
